I'm an assistant professor at Shanghai Center for Mathematical Science (SCMS), Fudan University. Before joining Fudan, I obtained my doctorate degree from Duke University and my bachelor's degree from the Hong Kong University of Science and Technology (HKUST).

Research summary:

What I've done (and will continue working on): Up until this point, my research has been primarily focused on the intricate challenges posed by 'learning in non-Euclidean space problems'. Broadly speaking, these challenges can be categorized into two primary types: global and local. At the global level, the inherent non-Euclideanness of these spaces disrupts the essential properties inherent to vector spaces. To address these complexities, I advocate for the adoption of metric space methods, which rely solely on the existence of an appropriate metric within the space. In particular, my coauthers and I have put forward several innovative methods that minimize batch costs and rely on minimal assumptions about the underlying space. Locally, the spaces we work with can sometimes resemble Euclidean spaces, but in these instances, the specification of coordinates becomes critical. Numerous algorithms are particularly sensitive to these coordinate specifications. Therefore, I actively promote the use of coordinate-robust methods whenever feasible, especially when estimating the Hessian. My coauthers and I have studied properties of coordinate-robust gradient/Hessian estimators.

What I'm working on (by learning it first): I am also interested in problems lie at the intersection of combinatorics, and probability theory. An example is properties of random binary tensors.
